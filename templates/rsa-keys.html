The RSA method is used to compute the public and private keys in an asymmetric cryptography system. The keys are represented by two pairs of values.

The first step consists of choosing two random prime numbers that are different from each other: a and b.

Next, you need to calculate the product n of the two prime numbers. The product n is the first element of the value pair in both keys.

$$ n = a \cdot b $$

If someone wanted to recover the private key using the product n shown in the public key, the only way to do so would be to guess both prime factors used to compute n. However, since these are prime numbers and typically very large, this process becomes extremely time-consuming.

Then, to compute both keys, it's necessary to calculate φ(n), which is the number of positive integers less than n that have only one common divisor with n, called "coprimes".

The public key component e is that coprime p between 1 and φ(n), excluding the endpoints, while the private key component d is the number p that, when multiplied by e and then taken module φ(n), gives a remainder of 1.

$$ e \rightarrow 1 \lt p \lt \phi(n) $$

$$ d \rightarrow (p \cdot e) \:\: mod \:\: \phi(n) $$

Therefore, the keys are:

  • Public key — (n, e)

  • Private key — (n, d)
